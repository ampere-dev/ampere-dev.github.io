Grid at Breaking Point: AI Data Centers Push U.S. Power System to the Limit

July 23, 2025

The surge in artificial intelligence (AI) is driving a massive increase in electricity demand, pushing America’s largest power grid, PJM Interconnection, to its limits. Serving 65 million people across 13 states and Washington, D.C., PJM has issued repeated “Maximum Generation” and “Load Management” alerts this summer as heatwaves and AI data centers strain capacity. With no spare power to meet this unprecedented demand, electricity prices are soaring, and the threat of blackouts looms. Here’s why the grid is maxed out and what it means for consumers.

AI Data Centers: A Power-Hungry Explosion

PJM’s territory includes Loudoun County, Virginia, dubbed “Data Center Alley,” the world’s largest hub for data centers. The rapid buildout of AI facilities is consuming electricity at an alarming rate, with modern server racks like Nvidia’s GB200 drawing 120 kW, double the 60–80 kW of older models. A single hyperscale data center can use 100–300 megawatts, enough to power tens of thousands of homes. The International Energy Agency (IEA) forecasts that global data center electricity use will double by 2026, reaching over 1,000 terawatt-hours, comparable to Japan’s annual consumption.

“There is simply no new capacity to meet new loads,” Joe Bowring, president of Monitoring Analytics, PJM’s independent watchdog, told Bloomberg. He suggests data center operators build their own power generation, as the grid struggles to keep up. This summer’s alerts, triggered by heat-driven air conditioning demand, underscore the system’s fragility as AI compounds the strain.

Retiring Plants and Decarbonization Challenges

The grid’s capacity crisis is worsened by the retirement of coal and nuclear plants, driven by decarbonization policies. While renewable energy is critical, only 15% of new capacity added in 2024 came from renewables, according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA), with natural gas and coal filling the gap. Over 20% of PJM’s generating capacity is slated to retire by 2030, leaving the grid vulnerable. In Maryland, officials warn of potential rolling blackouts, noting the state cannot rely solely on imported power to meet demand.

Escalating Costs: The Capacity Auction Squeeze

PJM’s annual capacity auction, which compensates power producers for ensuring supply, has driven costs to new heights. This year’s auction reached $16.1 billion, up from $14.7 billion last year, with the capacity price per megawatt-day hitting $329.17, compared to $269.92 in 2024. The auction’s price floor ($177.24) and cap ($329.17) cleared at the maximum, prompting criticism of its fairness. Consumers are feeling the pinch, with the EIA reporting a 15% rise in residential electricity prices over five years and projections of 20–30% increases in PJM’s region due to AI-driven demand.

Who Wins, Who Loses?

Independent power producers like Constellation Energy, Talen Energy, NRG, and Vistra are capitalizing on the demand surge, investing over $34 billion in 2025 to acquire natural gas plants. Natural gas, powering 40% of U.S. electricity, is a key bridge fuel as renewables lag. However, consumers face higher bills, with posts on X highlighting that data center demand accounts for a significant portion of cost increases. Jon Gordon of Advanced Energy United noted that data centers are outpacing the grid’s ability to adapt, deepening supply shortages.

The Broader Challenge: A Grid on the Brink

The North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC) warns that PJM’s region risks power shortages by 2027 if demand growth continues unchecked. Data centers could consume 20% of U.S. electricity by 2030, up from 4% today, per McKinsey & Company. Solutions like requiring data centers to generate their own power could fragment the grid, while the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) explores capacity market reforms, a process that may take years.

Looking Forward: Solutions or Shortages?

Short-term fixes include flexible load strategies, such as Duke University’s Nicholas Institute proposal to unlock 18 GW through brief curtailments. Long-term, the U.S. needs investment in nuclear power, renewables, and grid upgrades. PJM’s Dave Souder noted that 45,000 MW of approved energy projects are stalled by supply chain and permitting issues. Consumers can adopt energy-efficient appliances or join demand response programs, but systemic change is critical to avoid blackouts and unaffordable bills.

The AI revolution is transforming technology but exposing the U.S. grid’s vulnerabilities. Without urgent action, the balance between innovation and energy reliability will tip, leaving consumers to pay the price.

The AI Boom Is Sending Your Electricity Bills Through the Roof

July 22, 2025

The relentless rise of artificial intelligence (AI) is transforming industries, but it’s also driving an unprecedented surge in electricity demand across the United States. The nation’s largest power grid, PJM Interconnection, which serves 65 million people across 13 states and Washington, D.C., is struggling to keep up. As data centers powering AI applications multiply, electricity costs are soaring to record highs, and consumers are left footing the bill. Here’s a deep dive into why your utility bills are climbing and what it means for the future.

A Perfect Storm: AI Data Centers and Strained Grids

PJM Interconnection, which spans from the Midwest to the mid-Atlantic, recently announced that businesses and households will pay a staggering $16.1 billion for electricity capacity in the year starting June 2026. This marks a significant jump from last year’s record of $14.7 billion, with the capacity price per megawatt-day hitting an all-time high of $329.17, up from $269.92. The reason? A massive buildout of power-hungry AI data centers, particularly in areas like Loudoun County, Virginia—known as “Data Center Alley,” the world’s largest hub for data centers.

These data centers, which support the computational demands of AI models, consume electricity at levels comparable to small cities. For context, a single large data center can use 100–300 megawatts of power, enough to supply tens of thousands of homes. According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), global data center electricity consumption is projected to double between 2022 and 2026, reaching over 1,000 terawatt-hours—roughly equivalent to Japan’s annual electricity use.

Meanwhile, PJM has faced challenges this summer, issuing multiple “Maximum Generation” and “Load Management” alerts as heatwaves pushed air conditioning demand to the brink. Joe Bowring, president of Monitoring Analytics, PJM’s independent watchdog, told Bloomberg, “There is simply no new capacity to meet new loads.” This mismatch between supply and demand is exacerbating the crisis, as older power plants retire and investments in new generation and grid upgrades lag behind.

Skyrocketing Costs and the Auction Controversy

PJM’s annual capacity auction, which determines how much power producers are paid to ensure reliable electricity supply, has become a lightning rod for controversy. This year’s auction, the first to include both a price floor ($177.24) and cap ($329.17), cleared at the maximum, raising questions about whether the process truly reflects market dynamics. Last year’s auction saw a 600% spike in capacity prices, sparking outrage and leading to a settlement with Pennsylvania Governor Josh Shapiro to cap price increases for two years. Despite this, costs remain sky-high, with consumers in areas like Baltimore and Virginia facing rates as high as $466 and $444 per megawatt-day in previous auctions.

The financial impact is already hitting consumers. The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) reports that average residential electricity prices have risen by 15% over the past five years, and with AI-driven demand accelerating, further increases are likely. For households, this could mean monthly utility bills climbing by 20–30% in the coming years, particularly in regions served by PJM.

Winners and Losers in the AI Power Race

The surge in electricity demand has been a boon for independent power producers. Companies like Constellation Energy, Talen Energy, NRG, and Vistra have seen their stock prices soar, with Constellation and Talen surging in late trading following the auction results. As shown in the finance card above, Constellation Energy (CEG) closed at $323.70, Talen Energy (TAL) at $10.39, NRG at $160.55, and Vistra (VST) at $200.12 on July 23, 2025. These firms have invested over $34 billion this year alone, primarily acquiring natural gas plants to meet the AI-driven demand. Natural gas, which accounts for about 40% of U.S. electricity generation, is increasingly seen as a critical bridge fuel to power data centers while renewable energy projects struggle to scale.

However, consumers and small businesses are bearing the brunt of these costs. Jon Gordon, policy director at Advanced Energy United, noted that new data centers are coming online faster than the grid can adapt, exacerbating supply shortages. Meanwhile, investments in renewable energy and grid modernization are not keeping pace. The EIA estimates that only 15% of new electricity capacity added in 2024 came from renewables, with natural gas and coal plants filling the gap.

The Bigger Picture: Can the Grid Keep Up?

The AI boom is just one part of a broader challenge facing the U.S. power grid. Aging infrastructure, retiring coal plants, and delays in renewable energy projects are creating a perfect storm. The North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC) has warned that parts of the U.S., including PJM’s region, face a high risk of power shortages by 2027 if demand continues to outstrip supply. Data centers are expected to account for 20% of U.S. electricity consumption by 2030, up from 4% today, according to McKinsey & Company.

Proposed solutions, like requiring data center operators to build their own power generation, are gaining traction. However, this could lead to a patchwork of private energy systems, potentially undermining grid reliability and increasing costs further. On the policy front, regulators are under pressure to balance consumer protections with the need to incentivize new power generation. The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) is exploring reforms to capacity markets, but meaningful changes could take years.

What Can Consumers Do?

For now, households and businesses face tough choices. Energy efficiency measures, such as upgrading to energy-efficient appliances or installing smart thermostats, can help offset rising costs. Some states offer demand response programs, where consumers are paid to reduce usage during peak periods. However, these are short-term fixes for a systemic issue that requires long-term investment in clean energy and grid infrastructure.

The AI revolution is reshaping the world, but it’s also exposing the fragility of our energy systems. As data centers continue to proliferate, the tension between technological progress and energy affordability will only intensify. Without bold action, consumers may find themselves paying a steep price for a future powered by AI.

America’s Looming Electricity Crisis: Rationing on the Horizon?

July 20, 2025

As global electricity demand is projected to double over the next two decades, the United States faces an unprecedented challenge to its energy infrastructure. This growth, unseen since the post-World War II era, is driven by the rise of AI-driven data centers, advanced manufacturing, and the push for electric vehicles. However, the nation’s aging electrical grid and shifting energy policies are struggling to keep pace, raising concerns about potential electricity rationing in the future.

The Growing Demand and a Strained Grid

The U.S. Energy Department recently highlighted the severity of the issue in a report, noting that “electricity demand from AI-driven data centers and advanced manufacturing is rising at a record pace.” The report emphasizes that current approaches to grid management and load addition are insufficient to meet this surge. PJM Interconnection, the largest U.S. power grid operator serving 67 million customers across 13 states, is already feeling the strain. Electricity bills in parts of PJM’s territory are expected to rise by over 20% this summer, with businesses in Pennsylvania facing increases as high as 29%.

The rapid expansion of data centers, particularly in “Data Center Alley” in Northern Virginia, has exacerbated the problem. In PJM’s 2024 power grid auction, the cost for power plant capacity skyrocketed by 800%, jumping from $28.92 per megawatt-day in 2023 to $269.92 in 2024. This spike reflects the intense competition for power as tech giants secure capacity for their energy-hungry AI operations.

Policy Missteps and Energy Shortfalls

The current crisis has been years in the making, driven by energy policies that have prioritized short-term environmental goals over long-term reliability. Across the country, coal and nuclear power plants—reliable sources of baseload energy—have been shuttered, often replaced with less dependable solar and wind power. For example, New York’s Indian Point Power Plant was closed under former Governor Andrew Cuomo and Lieutenant Governor Kathy Hochul, a decision that has left the state scrambling to meet energy demands. Now, Governor Hochul is reportedly considering new nuclear power plants to address the shortfall.

The premature closure of fossil fuel plants, combined with restrictions on expanding natural gas production—despite the U.S. having abundant reserves—has left the nation vulnerable to supply shortages. PJM’s 2024 annual report cites these state-level energy policies as a significant factor in the current supply-demand imbalance.

China’s Rapid Power Plant Construction: A Contrast

While the U.S. struggles to meet rising electricity demand, China has taken a markedly different approach, rapidly expanding its power generation capacity to keep pace with its growing energy needs. In 2024 alone, China began construction on 94.5 gigawatts (GW) of new coal-fired power plants, the highest level in a decade, and resumed 3.3 GW of previously suspended projects. Since 2022, China has permitted an average of two large coal power plants per week, with 106 GW permitted in 2022 and 70.2 GW of new construction-initiated in 2023. This aggressive buildout, driven by energy security concerns and coal industry interests, has solidified coal’s role in China’s energy mix, despite its commitments to peak carbon emissions by 2030.

China’s nuclear power sector is also expanding at an unprecedented pace. As of 2024, China operates 58 nuclear power plants with a capacity of 60.88 GW, ranking third globally, and has 27 additional plants under construction, adding 32.31 GW. Since 2000, China has increased its operational nuclear reactors tenfold, with completion times for domestic reactor builds averaging five to seven years. For instance, the Hualong One reactor at Zhangzhou connected to the grid just 61 months after construction began. China’s ability to localize technology, streamline supply chains, and prioritize energy security has enabled it to build nuclear plants faster and more cost-effectively than most Western nations.

This rapid construction stands in stark contrast to the U.S., where regulatory hurdles, prolonged permitting processes, and a lack of cohesive energy policy have slowed power plant development. While China’s reliance on coal raises environmental concerns, its ability to quickly deploy both fossil and nuclear capacity highlights a strategic focus on meeting energy demand, offering a potential lesson for the U.S. as it grapples with its own infrastructure challenges.

The Consequences: Rising Costs and Potential Rationing

The immediate impact of this energy crunch is already being felt in consumers’ wallets. With electricity costs soaring, households and businesses alike are bracing for higher bills. In Pennsylvania, the situation is dire enough that the governor has threatened to abandon PJM’s services if prices cannot be stabilized. Meanwhile, PJM’s CEO, Manu Asthana, announced his resignation effective at the end of 2025, signaling internal turmoil within the organization.

Looking ahead, the combination of skyrocketing demand, an outdated grid, and insufficient new power generation raises the specter of electricity rationing. As policymakers push for widespread adoption of electric vehicles and issue warnings about “extreme heat” while urging consumers to limit air conditioning use, the irony is stark. The infrastructure needed to support these initiatives is simply not in place.

A Path Forward?

To avert a future of rationing and blackouts, the U.S. must act swiftly to modernize its electrical grid and expand reliable energy generation. This includes investing in new nuclear power plants, which provide consistent, carbon-free energy, and easing restrictions on natural gas development to bridge the gap while renewable technologies mature. Additionally, upgrading the nation’s grid infrastructure is critical to ensuring that power can be delivered efficiently to meet growing demand.

The challenges are immense, but they are not insurmountable. However, they require a pragmatic approach to energy policy—one that balances environmental goals with the need for affordability and reliability. Without swift action, Americans may face a future where electricity is not just more expensive but also rationed, with profound implications for daily life and economic growth.

Why the Alert Was Issued

The Maximum Generation Alert, accompanied by a NERC EEA Level 1 designation, reflects PJM’s proactive response to a high load forecast of 145,063 MW at 4 p.m. on July 16, driven by temperatures exceeding 90°F across its footprint, as forecasted by the NOAA Climate Prediction Center. This alert ensures all resources are committed to meet electricity demand, firm transactions, and reserve requirements, with concerns about sustaining contingency reserves. The Load Management Alert signals potential activation of demand response programs, where contracted customers reduce usage to alleviate grid stress. These measures are critical as PJM’s grid faces unprecedented pressure from rising demand, particularly from air conditioning during heatwaves, and a growing mismatch between supply and demand due to retiring fossil fuel plants.

Supply chain disruptions and tariffs further exacerbate shortages, leading to long lead times and increased costs, which threaten project timelines and grid reliability.

PJM’s recent actions, including the Department of Energy’s emergency order to keep Pennsylvania’s Eddystone Generating Station operational past its May 2025 retirement, highlight efforts to maintain reliability. However, the slow pace of new renewable projects, hampered by interconnection delays and local opposition, underscores the need for faster grid modernization and investment in firm baseload capacity, such as natural gas or nuclear, to bridge the gap.

Navigating the Build America, Buy America Act: A Guide for Infrastructure Projects

July 15, 2025

The Build America, Buy America Act (BABAA), enacted in November 2021 as part of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, represents a pivotal shift in federal procurement policy, aiming to bolster domestic manufacturing and supply chains. Effective since May 14, 2022, BABAA mandates that federally funded infrastructure projects—spanning roads, bridges, harbors, airports, and utilities—use products manufactured in the United States, with at least 55% of the component costs sourced domestically. This article delves into the intricacies of BABAA, its implications for the electrical transformer industry, and strategies for navigating compliance amidst supply chain challenges and rising tariffs.

Understanding BABAA Requirements

The BABAA’s core objective is to strengthen U.S. manufacturing by ensuring that federal funds support American-made products. For a product to be BABAA-compliant, it must be manufactured in the U.S., and over 55% of the total cost of its components must be mined, produced, or manufactured domestically, as outlined in Section 70912(6)(B)(ii) of the Act. This applies to all federally funded infrastructure projects, including those in the utility sector, where electrical transformers are critical components. Non-compliance can jeopardize project funding, as federal agencies are prohibited from granting funds to projects using non-compliant products. Limited waivers exist, but they are not guaranteed and can introduce significant delays, making proactive compliance essential.

Impact on Electrical Transformers

Electrical transformers, vital for power distribution in infrastructure projects, are classified as manufactured products under BABAA. This means that for projects receiving federal funding, transformers must meet the 55% domestic component threshold. The challenge lies in the globalized nature of transformer supply chains. In 2023, the U.S. imported approximately $27.3 billion worth of transformers, with only 18% of large power class transformers (over 100 MVA) manufactured domestically in 2019. The BABAA requirements, combined with tariffs on imported goods, have intensified demand for U.S.-made transformers, creating a bottleneck as domestic production struggles to keep pace. This scarcity limits vendor options, potentially increasing costs and extending project timelines.

Navigating Supply Chain Challenges

The transformer industry is grappling with a supply-demand imbalance, exacerbated by BABAA’s domestic sourcing mandates. The global supply chain disruptions, coupled with tariffs, have led to longer lead times and higher costs for raw materials and components. To address these challenges, companies are increasingly turning to remanufactured transformers, which are restored to their original operating conditions and can be produced more quickly than new units. This approach helps mitigate delays while ensuring compliance with BABAA standards. Additionally, the proposed CIRCUIT Act aims to alleviate supply constraints by offering tax credits to U.S. transformer manufacturers, incentivizing domestic production and potentially stabilizing the market.

Strategies for BABAA Compliance

To successfully navigate BABAA requirements, project managers and procurement teams should adopt a proactive approach. First, verify the compliance status of suppliers early in the project planning phase to avoid delays. Partnering with manufacturers that prioritize domestic sourcing and maintain robust inventories can streamline procurement. Second, explore remanufactured transformers as a cost-effective and BABAA-compliant alternative, particularly for urgent projects. Finally, stay informed about legislative developments, such as the CIRCUIT Act, which could expand domestic manufacturing capacity. Engaging with industry associations like the National Electrical Contractors Association (NECA) can also provide valuable insights and resources for compliance.

Economic and Industry Implications

BABAA’s focus on domestic manufacturing has far-reaching economic benefits, including job creation and increased tax revenue. Infrastructure projects using BABAA-compliant products contribute significantly to local economies, with construction activities generating substantial economic activity. However, the transition to domestic sourcing poses challenges, including higher initial costs and limited vendor availability. Over time, as domestic production scales up, these constraints are expected to ease, fostering a more resilient U.S. manufacturing base. The utility sector, in particular, stands to benefit from enhanced supply chain stability, ensuring reliable power infrastructure for communities nationwide.

Looking Ahead

The Build America, Buy America Act is reshaping the landscape of federally funded infrastructure projects, with significant implications for the electrical transformer industry. By prioritizing domestic manufacturing, BABAA aims to strengthen U.S. supply chains and support economic growth, but it also introduces challenges in a market already strained by demand and supply chain disruptions. By leveraging remanufactured transformers, staying informed about legislative incentives like the CIRCUIT Act, and partnering with compliant suppliers, project managers can navigate these complexities successfully. As the industry adapts, BABAA-compliant transformers will play a critical role in powering America’s infrastructure, ensuring both reliability and economic vitality.
