Inaccurate Commissioning Jeopardizing Your AI/HPC Data Center Rollout?

AI, HPC, and high-density liquid-cooled servers now routinely push rack powers to 50–100+ kW, generating extreme thermal loads that traditional air-cooled load banks simply cannot handle effectively. They dump massive heat and noise back into the room—causing hot-spot recirculation, test interruptions, skewed temperature/humidity readings, equipment stress, and unreliable results during commissioning.

More critically, air-cooled banks fail to test the actual liquid cooling infrastructure. Modern AI facilities rely on direct-to-chip or immersion systems that reject heat directly into chilled water/glycol loops (via CDUs, pumps, manifolds, and distribution). To validate real-world performance—flow rates, delta-T, pressure drops, transient response, pump efficiency, and backup modes—heat must be dissipated into the liquid system itself. Air-cooled testing misses these entirely, risking undetected issues that surface only after go-live.

Find the Right Configuration for Your Project

Aligned with ASHRAE S-Class Guidelines for Liquid Cooling

ASHRAE TC 9.9's S-classes define the supply temperature boundaries for the Technology Cooling System (TCS) — the coolant delivered directly to IT equipment like servers with direct-to-chip cold plates. These classes complement the older W-classes (for facility-side water) and account for the temperature rise (approach ΔT) across CDUs.

  • S20–S25: Lower inlet temps (~20–25°C) for high-performance or legacy-sensitive systems.
  • S30–S35: Common chiller/CDU setups (30–35°C).
  • S40–S45: Cooling tower/dry-cooler optimized (40–45°C) for energy efficiency.
  • S50+: Advanced waste-heat recovery or extreme free-cooling scenarios (50°C+).

Minimum TCS temperature is typically 2°C above room dew point to prevent condensation. Higher S-classes maximize liquid cooling benefits: reduced PUE, higher rack densities, quieter operation, and better heat capture — exactly what our 1 MW rental load banks help you validate during commissioning and resiliency testing.

Full details in the ASHRAE Datacom Encyclopedia (datacom.ashrae.org). Our units support testing across common S30–S45 ranges with precise flow/pressure/temp control.

Liquid Cooled Load Bank FAQs

Liquid cooled load banks are electrical devices that simulate electrical loads, allowing equipment such as generators, UPS systems, and power supplies to be tested under real-world conditions. Instead of air, these load banks use liquid (typically water or a coolant) to absorb and dissipate the heat generated during testing.

Liquid cooled load banks are commonly used in data centers, renewable energy systems, marine applications, oil & gas, and industrial sectors where large-scale power systems are employed and heat management is critical.

  • Validation: Many data centers now use liquid cooling and the load bank can be used during commissioning to validate those systems.
  • Low noise: As there are no fans there is very little noise during operation.
  • Smaller footprint: As there is no hot air discharge there isn’t a requirement of free space for dissipation.
